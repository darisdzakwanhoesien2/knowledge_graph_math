## Problem 1

Let 

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix}
2 & 10 \\
-11 & -5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

a) What is the operator norm of $A$?

b) Give the best rank-1 approximation to $A$.

### Solution 1a – Operator norm $\|A\| = \|A\|_2$

The operator norm is the largest singular value $\sigma_1$.

Compute $A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 125 & 70 \\ 70 & 125 \end{bmatrix}$, eigenvalues solve $\det(A^T A - \lambda I) = (\lambda-125)^2 - 4900 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda = 125 \pm 70$.

Thus $\sigma_1 = 195$, $\sigma_2 = 55$.

Therefore $\|A\| = 195$.

### Solution 1b – Best rank-1 approximation

By Eckart–Young theorem, the best rank-1 approximation is $\sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T$.

The eigenvector of $A^T A$ for $\lambda_1 = 195$ is $v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ (up to scaling).

The corresponding left singular vector $u_1 = A v_1 / \|A v_1\| = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ -16 \end{bmatrix}/20 = \begin{bmatrix} 3/5 \\ -4/5 \end{bmatrix}$.

Thus the best rank-1 approximation is

$$
195 \begin{bmatrix} 3/5 \\ -4/5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
= \begin{bmatrix} 117 & 117 \\ -156 & -156 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

(Or any scalar multiple that gives the same outer product.)

## Problem 2

Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ be of the form $A = I + u_1 v_1^*$ with $u_1, v_1 \in \mathbb{C}^n$ given. Find the inverse of $A$ as follows.

a) By solving the identity $(I + u_1 v_1^*)(I + \alpha u_1 v_1^*) = I$ with some $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$.

b) By finding the minimal polynomial of $A$ and using it to have the inverse.

### Solution 2a – Sherman–Morrison formula

Assume $v_1^* u_1 \neq -1$. Set $\alpha = -1/(1 + v_1^* u_1)$.

Then

$$
(I + u_1 v_1^*)(I + \alpha u_1 v_1^*) = I + (1 + \alpha v_1^* u_1) u_1 v_1^* = I,
$$

so

$$
A^{-1} = I - \frac{u_1 v_1^*}{1 + v_1^* u_1}.
$$

### Solution 2b – Minimal polynomial

The matrix $A = I + u_1 v_1^*$ has rank at most 2, so its minimal (and characteristic) polynomial has degree at most 2.

The eigenvalues are $1$ (multiplicity $n-1$) and $1 + v_1^* u_1$ (multiplicity 1).

Thus the minimal polynomial is

$$
m(\lambda) = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda - (1 + v_1^* u_1)).
$$

By Cayley–Hamilton $m(A) = 0$, so

$$
(A - I)(A - (1 + v_1^* u_1)I) = 0
\;\Rightarrow\;
(A - I)A = (v_1^* u_1) (A - I)
\;\Rightarrow\;
A - I = (v_1^* u_1) A^{-1} (A - I).
$$

Solving gives the same formula as above.

## Problem 3

Let 

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 5 \\
8 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Factor $A$ into the product of a Toeplitz matrix and a symmetric matrix.

### Solution

One possible factorization (many exist):

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 8 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 5 \\ 5 & 5 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

The first factor is lower triangular Toeplitz (constant on diagonals), the second is symmetric.

Another example:

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 4 \\ 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} 1/5 & 1 \\ 1 & 5 \end{bmatrix}
$$

(also Toeplitz × symmetric).

## Problem 4 – Sylvester equation

Let us consider the Sylvester equation

$$
A X - X B = C
$$

with $A, B, C \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$. Suppose you have diagonalized the matrices $A$ and $B$ as $A = Y \Lambda_1 Y^{-1}$ and $B = Z \Lambda_2 Z^{-1}$. How can you exploit these to solve the Sylvester equation in $O(n^3)$ flops?

### Solution

Substitute:

$$
Y \Lambda_1 Y^{-1} X - X Z \Lambda_2 Z^{-1} = C.
$$

Multiply from the left by $Y^{-1}$ and from the right by $Z$:

$$
\Lambda_1 (Y^{-1} X Z) - (Y^{-1} X Z) \Lambda_2 = Y^{-1} C Z.
$$

Let $\hat X = Y^{-1} X Z$. Then

$$
\Lambda_1 \hat X - \hat X \aec \Lambda_2 = Y^{-1} C Z.
$$

This is a system of $n^2$ scalar equations $(\lambda_i^{(1)} - \lambda_j^{(2)}) \hat x_{ij} = d_{ij}$, where $d_{ij}$ are entries of $Y^{-1} C Z$.

If the spectra of $A$ and $B$ are disjoint, all coefficients $\lambda_i^{(1)} - \lambda_j^{(2)} \neq 0$, so we can solve for each $\hat x_{ij}$ independently in $O(1)$ time.

Total cost: $O(n^3)$ for the two similarity transformations ($Y^{-1} C Z$) plus $O(n^2)$ for the divisions → overall $O(n^3)$ flops.

Then recover $X = Y \hat X Z^{-1}$ (another $O(n^3)$ but absorbed in the total).
